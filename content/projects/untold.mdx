## Overview

Untold is a digital monument to the memory of Mary Bradbury and the countless women targeted during the world Witch Hunt. Developed as part of a larger research initiative into invisible memories and counter-archive the project uses historical archival excavation to examine how power systems construct guilt and how communities resist it. The work centers on a visual conflict between two distinct histories: the red background text, representing the condemning narrative of the hegemonic jury, and the black text forming the word "WITCH”, composed of the actual testimonies of 115 people who risked their lives to defend Bradbury. By reclaiming the word as a symbol of power and communal support, the piece serves as a digital counter-archive for those silenced by the machinery of hegemonic systems and oppression.

## Process

The project was built using creative coding techniques with p5.js to create a dynamic typographic environment where the two narratives physically overlap and compete for the viewer’s attention. Using primary source documents from the University of Virginia’s Salem Witch Trials Archive, the code parses historical transcripts into a responsive visual interface. Untold explores the prism of forgotten memories, the idea that history is also often what remains unsaid or hidden behind official records. The large WITCH word acts as a reclaiming of this label, revealing that the true history of Mary Bradbury and other witches was one of remarkable social solidarity against a backdrop of systemic violence.
Created for the NYU courses "Medium of Memory" and "Programming from A to Z," the development process involved a rigorous curation of legal documents and communal petitions. I focused on the technical challenge of data-parsing historical text files into a format that could be manipulated in p5.js, ensuring that the sheer volume of the 115 testimonies was visually represented creatively. The aesthetic choice to use stark red and black was intentional, designed to mirror the blood of the trials against the truth. This iterative coding process allowed me to investigate how the digital space can act as a site for memorialization, turning a static database into a living, breathing conflict of memory.
